My commuter is a Decathlon Riverside — a practical step-through hybrid that handles daily duties: grocery runs, short errands, and the occasional evening spin around the neighborhood. It came with a Zoom suspension fork branded "63 COMFORT," which sounds reassuring until you realize it adds unnecessary weight and flex on smooth tarmac.

{{< foldergallery src="riverside-upgrade">}}

## Why Swap the Fork

The stock Zoom suspension fork is heavy, vague, and designed for terrain I never ride. On flat Singapore roads it just bobs under pedaling effort and adds dead weight. A rigid fork is the single best upgrade for a hybrid used primarily on pavement:

- **Weight savings** — carbon rigid fork vs. steel/alloy coil suspension is a noticeable difference.
- **Stiffer front end** — direct steering input, no dive under braking.
- **Less maintenance** — no seals to service, no stanchions to keep clean.

## The Fork: TOSEEK Carbon Rigid

I went with a TOSEEK carbon rigid fork from AliExpress. Key specs that had to match:

- **Steerer**: 1-1/8" (28.6mm), 300mm length — standard for most hybrids.
- **Axle-to-crown**: 470mm for 29" wheels — matches the stock fork geometry so the bike doesn't ride lower.
- **Brake mount**: Post mount for disc brakes (160mm rotor compatible).
- **Dropout**: 9mm quick-release — same as the original Zoom fork.
- **Tire clearance**: 70mm internal width — plenty for the 700x38C B'Twin Trekking Speed tires.

The fork came with an expander plug for the carbon steerer, which is the correct way to compress the headset (never use a star nut on carbon).

## Old vs. New

Placing both forks side by side, the difference is stark. The Zoom fork shows its age — rust on the stanchions, worn seals, and that trademark cheap suspension flex. The TOSEEK is clean, matte black carbon with a proper disc brake tab and thru-quality fit.

## Setup

The swap is straightforward if you have basic tools and a crown race setter:

1. Remove the front wheel, brake caliper, and stem.
2. Drop out the old fork, pull the crown race.
3. Set the crown race on the new fork (press fit — use a PVC pipe or proper tool).
4. Insert the new fork, stack spacers, tighten the stem.
5. Reinstall brake caliper — realign pads to the rotor.
6. Install the expander plug inside the carbon steerer before compressing the headset.

## Result

The bike feels noticeably lighter at the front and steers with more precision. Braking is more direct without the fork compressing. For a commuter that lives on tarmac and park connectors, it is the right call. The ride is marginally harsher over bumps, but nothing the 38mm tires at lower pressure cannot absorb.
